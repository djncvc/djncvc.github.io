Feedback, What After?

 Posted on March 6, 2021  |  3 minutes  |  

My job as a manager is to continuously provide feedback, and it always helps to improve on technique or remind myself of how to give and receive feedback properly. Recently, I went through a session with my colleagues about giving and receiving feedback. We talked about the importance of calibrating views on ourselves and others, and how feedback reduces potential blind spots. Group discussed the sandwich technique, wrap technique, and how giving options to the receiver improves probability of achieving the desired effect. Sometimes we want to give positive feedback to encourage people to continue a good job, or corrective feedback to change their behavior. However, there are certain situations in which corrective feedback on its own is not sufficient to achieve the desired effect. Consider the following two situations in which someone needed feedback:

  1. person was doing the right way and then started doing differently for worse
  2. person was doing wrong way all the time

In the first situation, it usually means that he needs to be reminded how the previous way was better. For example, the team agreed on Feature Branching, used it for a while, and suddenly one developer, for whatever reason, started committing code directly to the master branch. Assuming that feedback is received, correcting this shouldn’t be a such problem since that person used to do it “right way” before (for example: branch out, pull request, merge).

But the second situation is a different thing.

I am starting to exercise regularly

“I will drink 8 glasses of water each day”, “I’ll run a marathon”, “I wouldn’t eat junk food”, etc. I know what’s good for me, and promise to myself that I would do it and it doesn’t happen. Why? Because this is changing a habit. It’s not easy. Same with helping your colleague change his behavior. You gave feedback, but still, it’s hard to change his habits or change the way he did it before. Feedback is received and he realized what’s best for him but nothing happens. This is when you need to help that person. Especially if you are his manager. Don’t leave it just at feedback, or it probably wouldn’t be a success. You’ll end up giving corrective feedback again and again. Like I started to eat healthy more than dozen times.

This is good stuff, would be better if you tell us how

You can start with small things. For example, when both of you realize what needs to be done, you can ask your colleague to say it aloud. “I will branch out feature branches and merge through Pull Request”. People actually memorize better when they say it aloud. Its questionable does it creates better accountability but they will memorize better.
You can be their accountability buddy. They can pay you lunch for each missed instance (win-win), you can setup regular checkins, goals, acknowledge progress, etc. If you are a manager then you can check what you can do to make it easy to stick with new behavior. Like, introduce better tooling to branch/merge. If it’s a big change, then go from small steps to larger ones.

Preach what you teach

You need to ask yourself how good you are with changing your own habits. If you are throwing feedback around, and your behavior stays the same most of the time, then it is highly unlikely that you will have a significant impact on your colleagues. If you want to read more about changing habits I would recommend an excellent book by James Clear: Atomic Habits. It could help you change your and other’s habits.
